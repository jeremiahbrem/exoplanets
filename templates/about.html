About Exoplanet App

Learn About Other Star Systems

You now have access to NASA's extensive exoplanet database, with over 4000 confirmed planets. You'also learn about the different properties of planets and their host stars, and how they compare to our own solar system. For most planet-star systems, if the data is available, you'll see visual simulations of how the host star size and color compare to the sun, as well as some exoplanet size comparisons to the earth.

Create Planet Lists

Start off your journey by creating a list to add some of your favorite planets found in your search. Create different lists for different search categories for particular planet or star properties you are looking for. For example, you could create a list called "Hot stars", with a description "Host stars with surface temps over 10000K".

Search Planets

Search exoplanets by name, host star name, or by single or multiple parameters. Check the options you want to search, and narrow your results by limiting values (optional). The results table will include all planets that meet your criteria and will include values for the parameters you've checked. Select a list and add your favorite planets.

Parameter Options
In Habitable Zone

The habitable zone of a star includes the minimum and maximum boundaries within which liquid water can be present, and could theoretically have the potential to host life. If an exoplanet in NASA's database includes star optical magnitude, star spectral type, distance, and planet orbit, then the habitable zone has been calculated.

Planet Mass

Planet mass is like the weight of the planet, or the amount of material gravitationally bound together. It is measured in earth masses, with a value of 1 equal to the mass of our own planet.

Planet Radius

Planet radius is a measure of the size of the planet, and a value of 1 is equal to 1 earth radius, or 6378 km.

Planet Orbit

A planet's orbit data is the semi-major axis of the planet's orbit around it's host star. It is measured in astronomical units(AU), with 1 AU being the distance from the earth to the sun, or about 150 million km.

Number of Planets in System

This parameter indicates the number of planets in a particular exoplanet's star system. Many stars, like the sun, have multiple planets that orbit them.

Distance

The distance for an exoplanet indicates the distance from our solar system to the host star. It is measured in parsecs, with 1 parsec equal to about 3.26 light years, or about 19 trillion miles.

Host Star Mass

Star mass is like the weight of the star, or the amount of material gravitationally bound together. It is measured in solar masses, with a value of 1 equal to the mass of our own sun.

Host Star Radius

Host star radius is a measure of the size of an exoplanet's host star. It is measured in solar radii, with 1 solar radius being the radius of the sun, or 696340 km.

Host Star Surface Temp

The surface temperature of a star is measured in kelvin(K). The surface temperature of our sun is 5778 K, or 9941 degrees Fahrenheit.

Host Star Spectral Type

The spectral type of a star is a classification of the star's light spectrum characteristics. Most stars are classified as O, B, A, F, G, K, and M, with a number from 0 to 9, in order from hottest to coolest. Most spectral types also include a luminosity class, designated by Ia, Iab, Ib, IIb, II, III, IV, V, sd, or D, in order from most to least luminous. The luminosity of a star is the amount of energy emitted from the star. The spectral type of the sun is G2V.

The B-V index is a measure of the visual color appearance of a star. A high index indicates a star with a cooler surface temperature and more reddish in color. A low index indicates a star with a hotter surface temperature and more bluish in color. Sometimes a hot, blue star may be obscured with dust and therefore appear more reddish. For most planets on this site, you'll see a beautiful color representation of the appearance of the host star compared to our sun, which has a B-V index of 0.656 and appears yellow-white in color.

Star Optical Magnitude

A stars optical magnitude is a measure of the visual brightness of the star as it appears to the naked eye. A high number indicates a dimmer star, while a lower number indicates a brighter star. The naked eye limit in good seeing conditions is about 6 or 7, while the limit with a 5 inch telescope is about 13. The brightest star in the night sky is Sirius, with an optical magnitude of -1.46. Venus, the brightest planet, can reach -4.
